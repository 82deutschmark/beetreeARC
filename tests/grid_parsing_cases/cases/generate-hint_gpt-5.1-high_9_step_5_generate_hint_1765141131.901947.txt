From the examples and the hints, the key ideas seem to be:

1. **Identify large background-like regions.**  
   In all three grids, there are big, mostly uniform areas that don’t contain the “interesting” multi-colored structures.

2. **Keep the region where the compact, mixed-color components live.**  
   In the test input, all of the non–background colors (2,4,5,6,7,8,9,0) appear only in the *lower* part of the grid, embedded in a big field of color `1`.  
   The upper part (rows 1–10) is mostly color `3` and `1` with no such components, and the very last row (row 25) is just all `1`.

3. **Choose the uniform background from the prominent object region.**  
   In the lower part that actually contains the interesting structures, the background is already uniformly `1`.

4. **Crop away the pure background-only rows.**  
   - All “interesting” non-background colors are confined to rows 12–24.  
   - Rows 1–11 contain only the large background-like areas with no compact components.  
   - Row 25 is also pure background (`1`s only).  
   So the minimal rectangle that contains all of the desired components is rows 12–24 and the full width of the grid.

5. **The resulting region already has the compact components on a uniform background, so no further rearrangement is needed.**  
   Taking rows 12–24 gives a 13×25 grid where:
   - The background is uniformly color `1`.
   - All the multi-colored components (in colors 9,6,8,4,2,5,7,0) are preserved with their shapes and relative arrangement.
